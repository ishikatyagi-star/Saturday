--- NEWTON'S FIRST LAW (LAW OF INERTIA) ---
- An object stays at rest or moves with constant velocity unless an external force acts on it
- Inertia = resistance to change in motion
- More mass = more inertia
- When net force = 0, velocity is constant (includes being at rest)
- Common mistake: thinking force is needed to KEEP things moving (it's not — in space, things move forever without force)

Real-world examples:
- Car accelerating → you feel pushed back into seat
- Car braking → you lurch forward
- Heavy truck hard to push AND hard to stop
- Seatbelts prevent inertia from throwing you through windshield

--- NEWTON'S SECOND LAW (F = ma) ---
- F = ma (Force = mass × acceleration)
- Acceleration ∝ Force (directly proportional)
- Acceleration ∝ 1/mass (inversely proportional)
- Unit: Newton (N) = kg·m/s²
- Direction: acceleration is in direction of net force
- Free body diagram → find net force → apply F = ma

Problem solving method:
1. Draw free body diagram
2. List all forces
3. Find net force (vector sum)
4. Apply a = F_net / m
5. Check units!

Example: 5 kg block, 20 N force, no friction → a = 4 m/s²
With 5 N friction: a = (20-5)/5 = 3 m/s²

--- NEWTON'S THIRD LAW (ACTION-REACTION) ---
- Every action has an equal and opposite reaction
- Forces come in pairs
- Key fact: act on DIFFERENT objects → they do NOT cancel

Examples:
- Walking: foot pushes ground back, ground pushes foot forward
- Chair: you push chair down, chair pushes you up
- Rocket: exhaust pushed down, rocket pushed up
- Hammer and nail: hammer pushes nail down, nail pushes hammer up

Exam tip: always identify what object receives each force in the pair.
